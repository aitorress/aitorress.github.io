# Revision Plan: Structure Compresses the Value Loop

Prioritized revision items, highest ROI first. Galloway and Housel converged on items 1–4; items 5–7 are individual recommendations the author should weigh against the piece's intent.

## 1. Cut or compress §1 ("What I'm Claiming, and What I'm Not")
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Both critics flag §1 as throat-clearing. The thesis ("AI has raised the per-person floor enough that flat, tiny teams with decision authority inside the team are newly viable at meaningful scale") lives in paragraph two of §1, behind ~120 words of "I want to be honest" and Ng-hedging. The cold open earns momentum; §1 spends it on apology.
- **Why it matters:** Readers form their commitment to a piece in the first 30 seconds after the cold open. Right now those 30 seconds are spent reassuring the reader the author is humble. Galloway hates the hedging; Housel hates the distrust of the metaphor. Same diagnosis from both sides.
- **Suggested fix:** Move the thesis sentence to immediately after the cold open. Keep the Ng acknowledgment as a single parenthetical or a short sentence near the end of the new combined opening. Keep the scope note ("This essay assumes you are on a team that has, or can hire, high-agency individuals"). Cut the rest.
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Drop the §1 heading entirely, or repurpose it for the new combined opening?
  - Is the Ng acknowledgment load-bearing for credibility, or can it move to a footnote at the bottom of the post?

## 2. Stop the close one paragraph earlier
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Both critics flag the close. The strongest beat is "Every ship is a training example. The team is the model. Each iteration sharpens the next one, or it doesn't." Then the piece keeps going for another four sentences of structural restatement and a "compounding has a direction" line. Galloway calls the close soft ("fortune cookie"); Housel calls it rhetorical cleanup the metaphor does not need.
- **Why it matters:** The last sentence is what the reader carries to the next day. Right now the metaphor lands and then gets covered up. The piece ends on instruction ("make sure your direction is one you would have chosen"), not on the image.
- **Suggested fix:** Two options the author should pick between:
  - **Housel cut:** End with "Each iteration sharpens the next one, or it doesn't." Delete everything after. The discomfort of the disjunction is the close.
  - **Galloway sharpen:** Keep going past the metaphor but name the cost. "The compounding is real, and it has a direction. The people whose direction is not one they would have chosen are the people the people who picked walk past."
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Which lens — Housel's "stop sooner" or Galloway's "name the cost" — fits the piece's intent?
  - Does the senior-IC reader need the call-to-action, or do they already have the move?

## 3. Cut the "two readers" meta-line in §6
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Section 6 contains the sentence "This is the part of the essay where the K-shape arrives, and where the two readers I have been writing to need to hear different things." Both critics flag this. Galloway sees hedging; Housel sees the magician explaining the trick. Either way it breaks the spell at the section where the spell most needs to hold.
- **Why it matters:** The two-reader voice is a craft decision that works when invisible. Naming it makes it self-conscious. The senior engineer and the mid-coordinator both read the same paragraphs; both find what they need without being told that is what is happening.
- **Suggested fix:** Delete the sentence outright. Rewrite the transition so the K-shape arrives without announcement. Candidate: "Here is where the K-shape arrives. If you are a senior engineer who has been doing this on your own…" Cuts the meta and keeps the bridge.
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Is the transition strong enough without any bridge sentence?

## 4. Sharpen the Zoom scene in the cold open
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Galloway flags "four people take turns saying things the rest of us already knew" as too soft. Housel notes the standup scene is the one that sticks; the Zoom scene is good but a touch longer than it needs to be. Same target from both angles.
- **Why it matters:** The two scenes set the entire piece. The standup scene currently does heavier work than the Zoom scene. Closing the gap raises the cold open's ceiling.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "saying things the rest of us already knew" with something concrete and dated. Candidates:
  - "reading the same slide that landed in Slack three days ago"
  - "going through bullet points half the room already skimmed yesterday"
  - "presenting numbers that have been in the dashboard all week"
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Which specific image best matches the kind of meeting the author has actually been in?
  - Is there an even sharper second-scene to swap in (a quarterly review with no decision, a roadmap meeting that re-litigates settled work)?

## 5. Add quantification (Galloway lens)
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Galloway flags that Midjourney's "hundreds of millions with eleven people" is the only stat in the piece. The K-curve is asserted; the cycle-time claim is asserted; the per-person ceiling claim is asserted. None are quantified.
- **Why it matters:** A synthesis piece with one stat reads as opinion. Two or three well-placed numbers move it toward observation.
- **Suggested fix:** Find specific homes for two or three of:
  - A cycle-time delta from the author's own seat (pre-AI vs. now). Even an approximation ("a feature that used to take three sprints now takes one") is concrete.
  - The size of Uber's Programs and Platforms group, or a number on how many quarterly reviews have killed vs. saved teams.
  - A Claude Cowork user-count or ship-velocity figure if Felix has shared one publicly.
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Which numbers can the author defend in print? (Don't add a stat that needs hedging.)
  - Is the piece's synthesis voice diluted by adding stats, or sharpened?

## 6. Deepen §4 (Housel lens)
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Housel flags §4 as the spine the author has not yet let themselves write. Basecamp / early Linear / 37signals get one sentence each. The historical falsifier — "what 2018 couldn't do" — is named but not earned.
- **Why it matters:** §4 is the section that prevents the piece from being lean-startup-in-AI-clothing. If it's thin, the whole thesis wobbles. Two patient paragraphs would let the falsifier do its work.
- **Suggested fix:** Pick one 2018 team (likely Basecamp) and spend a paragraph on what they specifically figured out, what they hit a wall on, and what would have changed for them in 2026. Keep the other two as one-line confirmations.
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Is Basecamp the strongest example, or is early Linear (more recent, closer to current peers)?
  - Does deepening §4 require new research, or can it be written from existing knowledge?

## 7. Consider the framing trade — title and structure (Housel lens vs. Galloway lens)
**Status:** pending

- **Issue:** Galloway and Housel disagree on the load-bearing line:
  - Galloway: "Hire well or manage forever" is the strongest line. Move it up. Title candidate.
  - Housel: "Every ship is a training example; the team is the model" is the metaphor the essay is secretly about. Lead with it.
- **Why it matters:** Both candidates are stronger than the current title ("Structure Compresses the Value Loop") on different axes. Galloway's pick is punchier; Housel's is more haunting.
- **Suggested fix:** Decide late, after items 1–4 are resolved. The current title is defensible; the question is whether either alternative does more work.
- **Questions to resolve:**
  - Which axis matters more for this piece's audience: punch (Galloway) or haunt (Housel)?
  - Does keeping the current title and surfacing both candidate lines inside the body solve the trade?
