This is one of the coolest breads to make. It's the bread behind the famous sandwiches at All'Antico Vinaio in Florence (and now all over the world), and it's the snobbiest cousin of the focaccia everyone knows and loves, made on purpose for sandwiches. It has all the great things you want for a sandwich. It's a two-day process and you need to be very precise, which is what I don't love about making bread, but believe me, in this particular case it's worth it.

## Ingredients

For the poolish (day one):

- 1 g active yeast
- 250 g bread flour
- 250 g warm water, around 90°F

For the dough (day two):

- 500 g all-purpose flour
- 250 g warm water
- 6 g salt
- Olive oil, a lot of it, throughout
- Flaky salt, for the top
- Rosemary or whatever herbs you like, for the top (optional)

## Steps

1. Day one: make the poolish, the pre-fermented starter dough. In a large bowl, using a scale, measure out 1 g of active yeast and 250 g of bread flour and mix them together. Then add 250 g of warm water, around 90°F. This needs to be incredibly precise. If not, it won't work. Mix until well combined, cover the bowl with plastic wrap, and let it sit at room temperature for 12 hours.

2. After the 12 hours you should have a poolish that doubled in size and is both bubbly and jiggly. That's about 500 g of starter going into day two.

3. Add the 500 g of all-purpose flour, 250 g of warm water, and 6 g of salt to the poolish. Use a spatula to combine everything until the dough is workable, then knead with your hands. Add olive oil to avoid stickiness instead of flour. This is how you work a dough this high in hydration.

4. Shape it into a raggedy ball, add more olive oil, and let it rest for a couple of hours. Every 30 minutes or so, do a set of stretch and folds: grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over itself, then go around the bowl doing the same. This is the kneading for a dough this wet, and it's how you build the gluten. Each round you'll feel it get smoother and stronger. Leave it alone for the last 30 minutes.

5. After the final 30 minutes of resting, drop the dough seam side down onto a sheet tray previously spread with olive oil. Now do what the bread is named for: schiacciata, pressing little divots into the top of the dough. You don't want to stretch it all the way to the ends of the tray. It will relax and get there eventually. Just enough stretching so it gets some shape. Drizzle more olive oil on top.

6. Two ways from here. If you want this pre-prepared for guests tomorrow, wrap it up and put it in the fridge; the next day, take it out and let it rest a couple of hours. If today was the plan all along, just let it rest wrapped for two more hours at room temperature.

7. After those two hours of rest, same day or out of the fridge, do another schiacciata. This time spread the dough out as much as possible over the tray. Schiacciata is thinner than focaccia, and that's exactly what makes it perfect for sandwiches. Let it rest for two more hours.

8. Preheat the oven to 450°F. Before the dough goes in, brush more olive oil on top and throw on some flaky salt. This is where you can get creative and have fun with whatever flavors or herbs you want. If you're adding something like rosemary, add it closer to the end of the cook so it doesn't burn. Completely up to you.

9. Bake for somewhere between 15 and 25 minutes, but you're not going by time. You're going by color and texture, so keep checking on it and rotate the tray halfway through. You want it nice and crispy.

10. Out of the oven, one last dab of olive oil.
